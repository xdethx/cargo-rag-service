MAKROSHIP — SHIPPING & CUSTOMS FAQ
(Sample data for RAG testing. Plain-text format on purpose, to exercise the .txt loader.)

Q: Why is my shipment stuck at customs?
A: A shipment usually sits at customs (status CUS-HOLD or CUS-INSP) for one of three reasons:
the paperwork is incomplete, the declared value looks inconsistent with the contents, or the
goods were selected for a random physical inspection. The most common single cause is a
missing or unclear commercial invoice. If a document is missing, the importer is contacted to
supply it before the shipment can move.

Q: How long does customs clearance take?
A: A straightforward clearance (status CUS-SUB to CLR) is often the same day or one business
day. A documentary hold (CUS-HOLD) typically adds 2 to 5 business days. A physical inspection
(CUS-INSP) typically adds 3 to 7 business days. Paying any assessed duty promptly is the
single biggest thing the importer can do to avoid extra delay.

Q: Who pays the customs duty and VAT?
A: By default the importer (the receiver) pays import duty and VAT. The exception is when the
shipment is sent under the Incoterm DDP (Delivered Duty Paid), in which case the sender has
agreed to cover those charges. The Incoterm chosen at the time of sale decides who pays.

Q: What happens if I do not pay the import charges?
A: The shipment stays in DUTY-DUE status and is not released. After a holding period
(commonly 10 to 15 calendar days, depending on the destination) the parcel is returned to the
sender with status RTN, and return transport costs may be deducted.

Q: How is the duty amount calculated?
A: Duty is based on the customs value of the goods (usually the price on the commercial
invoice, sometimes plus freight and insurance), the product's tariff classification (its HS
code), and the country of origin. VAT is then applied on top. Because the HS code and origin
both affect the rate, an accurate invoice and a certificate of origin can change the final
amount.

Q: Can I track my shipment, and what do the status codes mean?
A: Yes. Every shipment has a tracking number issued at the REG (Registered) stage. The status
codes follow the journey from pickup through transit, customs, and delivery. See the
"Customs & Shipment Status Glossary" document for the meaning and typical duration of each
code.

Q: My tracking shows EXC (Exception). What does that mean?
A: EXC is a generic flag that something interrupted the shipment — a bad or incomplete
address, a weather or transport disruption, a damaged shipping label, or a failed delivery
attempt. The tracking note next to the EXC scan usually states the specific reason and the
next action.

Q: My parcel arrived damaged. What should I do?
A: If the contents are damaged, keep all packaging and take photos, then open a claim within
the carrier's claim window (commonly 7 to 14 days from delivery). If damage is spotted in the
network before delivery, the parcel is moved to DMG status and assessed before it continues.

Q: Are gifts exempt from duty?
A: Not automatically. Most countries allow a small "de-minimis" value below which no duty is
charged, but gifts above that threshold are taxed like any other import. A gift shipment still
needs a proforma invoice that describes the contents and states a value.

Q: What is an A.TR certificate and do I need one?
A: An A.TR is a movement certificate used for goods moving between Türkiye and the European
Union under their customs union. Presenting a valid A.TR lets the importer claim a reduced or
zero duty rate. It is optional, but without it the standard duty rate applies.
